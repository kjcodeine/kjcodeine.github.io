Menominee

The Menominee people have always lived in Wisconsin around the Green Bay area. They refer to themselves as Mamaceqtaw, meaning “The People,” but other tribes refer to them as manomin, an Ojibwe word for wild rice, their main food source.

After the War of 1812, the Oneida, Stockbridge-Munsee, and Brothertown tribes were forced onto their land to escape colonization. While they formed their own land treaties, the United States government intervened, taking much of the land and leaving the 4 tribes all but a portion. After two more treaties, the 1836 Treaty of the Cedars and 1848 Treaty of Lake Poygan, the Minominee sold their land to the United States, in exchange for land along the Crow Wing River in Minnesota. However, in 1852, the President allowed the Menominee to stay on a temporary reservation in northeastern Wisconsin, made permanent by an 1854 treaty.

The language has been severely threatened in the past, only kept alive by elders, but current revitalization efforts within the tribes have kept it alive and well.
